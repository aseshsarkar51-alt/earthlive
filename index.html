Our story

Some things deserve to be written down.

EarthLive brings back the memory book — a digital space where your people can add their thoughts, memories, and heartfelt messages. No feed to scroll, no strangers, no noise. Just words worth keeping.

Here, your page is your book. You choose the questions, send one link, and your people fill it with words you'll actually want to keep. You approve every entry before it appears, and you decide whether your book is open to the world or just for you.

Your book

Make your book and pick a question set — classic, deep, funny, or farewell. Share your link anywhere. Every signature waits for your approval before it's posted, and each entry pins to a memory map, so you can see where your people wrote from.

Your diary

Not everything is meant to be read by others. Your diary is a private space that belongs to you alone — write freely, and nobody sees a word of it unless you decide otherwise. Each entry is private by default. When you want to, you can send one to a single person like a letter, or add it to your own book. Sharing is always a choice, never the default.

Your events

Reunions, farewells, weddings, birthdays. Create an event page and share a link or QR code with the people who were there. You approve who joins. Inside, members write about each other, add photos, tag friends, and pin memories to a shared map — a book the whole group fills together.

What we're not

There's no endless feed here, no follower count to chase, no algorithm deciding what you see. EarthLive is quiet on purpose. It's meant to be a place you visit, feel something, and close — not somewhere you get stuck.

Digital connection, loneliness,
and the case for memory

A synthesis of the evidence behind EarthLive.

I. The epidemiology of disconnection

Contemporary societies present a well-documented paradox: unprecedented communicative capacity coexisting with historically elevated loneliness. The WHO Commission on Social Connection (2025) estimates that one in six individuals globally is affected by loneliness, associated with approximately 871,000 deaths annually. Contrary to the received framing of loneliness as a geriatric condition, prevalence is inverted by age: 79% of adults aged 18–24 meet the threshold on the UCLA Loneliness Scale, the highest of any cohort (Cigna, 2023). Longitudinal meta-analytic evidence spanning 345 studies and roughly 125,000 participants indicates that each successive young-adult generation reports greater loneliness than the one preceding it. Concurrently, reported close-friendship networks have contracted from approximately five confidants to two within a few decades — a phenomenon termed the friendship recession.

II. Behavioural withdrawal from platforms

User behaviour is responding to this condition in measurable ways. Survey data from 2025 indicate that 52% of Generation Z attempted to discontinue social media use, with mental health cited as the primary motivation; 47% of general respondents report having deleted a social application due to stress or anxiety, rising to 56% among Gen Z (Incogni, 2026). Critically, the dominant behavioural pattern is not exit but retreat into controlled participation: reduced posting frequency, restricted audiences, and secondary pseudonymous accounts — a shift from broadcast to bounded disclosure. Qualitative accounts converge on a loss of perceived ownership over the mediated self.

III. Mechanisms of harm

The literature locates the harm not in mediated communication per se but in its modality. The passive-use hypothesis (Verduyn et al., 2017) holds that non-interactive consumption of curated content depresses subjective wellbeing through upward social comparison and envy. A meta-analysis of 141 studies (N ≈ 145,000; Journal of Computer-Mediated Communication, 2024) found passive use associated with worse emotional outcomes in general platform contexts, whereas active, directed use predicted greater online support (r = .34), wellbeing, and positive affect. The operative variable, in short, is posture: spectatorship toward weak ties corrodes; addressed communication within strong ties sustains. Case evidence from BeReal further demonstrates that authenticity-framed platforms are not exempt — Thomas and Talbot (2025) document a trajectory from enthusiasm to discontinuance driven by performativity, temporal demand, and feature creep, even as participants valued the platform precisely as a digital memory archive.

IV. Shared remembering as a protective factor

A distinct research programme identifies a countervailing mechanism. Two decades of experimental work on nostalgia (Wildschut et al., 2006; Sedikides & Wildschut, 2025) establish it as a predominantly positive, social emotion with reliable psychological functions: elevated self-esteem, increased meaning in life, and strengthened social connectedness. Zhou, Sedikides, Wildschut, and Gao (2008) demonstrate a homeostatic loop — loneliness triggers nostalgic recall, and nostalgic recall in turn restores perceived connectedness, counteracting the initiating state. Downstream, nostalgia fosters self-continuity, the perceived coherence of identity across time, which mediates eudaimonic wellbeing. Notably, nostalgic narratives characteristically feature the self as protagonist embedded in a social context: the protective memory is, almost by definition, a memory containing one’s people.

V. Design implication

Taken together, these findings specify the architecture of a less harmful — indeed, a restorative — social technology: one that eliminates the conditions for passive spectatorship and upward comparison (no feed, no metrics, no weak-tie audience) while maximising addressed communication within strong ties and the accumulation of retrievable shared memory. EarthLive is an application of this specification: a bounded, owner-moderated memory book populated exclusively by known others, whose stored contents constitute precisely the class of stimuli the nostalgia literature identifies as protective. Its hypothesis follows directly from the evidence reviewed here — that for the loneliest connected generation, the corrective to being endlessly seen is an instrument for being durably known.

Privacy Policy

Last updated: July 2026

What we collect

When you create a book: your name, bio, profile and cover photos, chosen questions, optional social links, and your email or mobile number if you sign up. When you sign someone's book: your name, answers, message, and any photos you attach. When you write in your diary: the text and title you write. When you create or join an event: the event details, your posts, photos, and comments. We also collect basic technical data (device, browser, approximate location) to keep the service working and secure.

How we use it

To run EarthLive: showing your book to people you share it with, delivering signatures to book owners for approval, keeping your diary private to you, and running event pages for their members. We do not sell your personal data. We do not show your private pages to anyone but you.

Who can see what

Public books are visible to anyone with the link and may appear in search. Private books show only your name, bio, and photos — the entries are visible only to you. Signatures never appear publicly until the book owner approves them.

Your diary is private by default and visible only to you. An entry becomes visible to someone else only when you explicitly choose it: sharing an entry with one person makes it readable by that person alone, and adding an entry to your book makes it visible to whoever can see your book. You can change an entry back to private at any time, which removes it from your book. Nobody at EarthLive reads your diary.

Event pages are visible only to approved members. The event host approves every person who joins, and can remove members or posts. People who aren't members see only the event name and member count.

Your choices

You can edit or delete your book, decline or remove any signature, delete any diary entry, leave or delete events, switch between public and private at any time, and request deletion of your account and data by writing to hello@earthlive.in. We will act on verified requests within 30 days.

Children

EarthLive is not intended for children under 13 (or the higher minimum age in your region). If you believe a child's data has been collected, contact us and we will remove it.
